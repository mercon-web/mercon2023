Professor Weeratunge Malalasekera

Professor of Computational Fluid Flow and Heat Transfer
Wolfson School of Mechanical Electrical and Manufacturing Engineering

Renewable Energy and the Role of Hydrogen as a Future Fuel for Clean Cities

Due to world population growth, growing industrial development, improvements in living standards, emerging technologies (such as electric vehicles) and the growth in the domestic and industrial use of modern equipment, the current growing trend in electricity demand and consumption will continue long into the future. The management of electrical energy supply to meet the growing demand without contributing more to the greenhouse gas problem is a major task. To reduce greenhouse gases, more and more renewable sources of electrical energy are being added to networks. However, these new sources of supplies can be intermittent and variable. Wind, for example, is a valuable source of green energy but its availability is very variable. Solar energy is available during the daytime subjected to sunshine but it is not available during the night. Usually, the peak demand of electricity for industrial customers is during the daytime and the peak demand for domestic customers is during early evenings.

Growth and sustainability of future renewable energies, therefore, requires the management of electricity networks using smart grid technologies. It also requires options to store energy and regenerate during demand periods. On a small scale, options are required to have self-contained integrated systems to store energy. Hydrogen in this context is considered to be the clean fuel of the future which does not contribute to greenhouse gases. Moreover, hydrogen has been recognised as a possible energy storage medium. Excess electricity from wind and solar could be converted to hydrogen by electrolysis. Depending on the application, hydrogen could be stored using various techniques.

In this lecture, the role of hydrogen in the context of renewable energy is explained and the methods of hydrogen storage options are discussed. Properties of hydrogen as a fuel are further explained, as are its advantages and disadvantages. Research undertaken into hydrogen storage systems, combustion and safety aspects of hydrogen are further highlighted.

Prof. J. Katupitiya

University of New South Wales, Australia

Autonomous Vehicles - The Outlook

The expectation of the imminent arrival of the autonomous vehicles into our lives has led to raging debates around the world about their ethical and technological aspects. The opinions about what it is, is widely varied. At the same time the researchers and the developers ranging from academia to major car manufacturers are fast advancing towards the introduction of some form of autonomous vehicle into the market. As such, now is a good time to discuss, the possible scenarios of future autonomous vehicles. The presenter will present the autonomous field vehicles his team has developed over the years for the agricultural, mining and road construction sector and then will shed some light on the possibilities for the public transport sector. Finally, he will present some challenges to the audience to stimulate their thinking to understand the outlook for the autonomous vehicles on our roads.

Prof. Nandana Rajathewa

University of Oulu, Finland

Wireless Communications â€“ 5G to 6G

We are now in the first stage of ushering in 5G communication systems. While the initial focus will be broadband services named as enhanced mobile broadband (eMBB), other more industry driven applications are also gaining ground. Main among those is the use of ultra-reliable low latency communications (URLLC) targeting applications such as Industrial Internet-of-Things (IoT) and eHealth. It is also expected that a large number of devices will be connected to the wireless networks which necessitate innovative methods of granting access and being energy efficient. The current standardization addresses these to a certain extent with frequency allocations in sub 6 GHz and going into mmWave region starting from 28 GHz and above. Going into mmWave though brings more bandwidth, also results in several challenges due to narrow beam widths and a large number of antenna elements to be used. Given the variety of applications anticipated such as VR, AR, MR which are now known as XR technologies we need to think about moving capabilities from central cloud towards the edge of the network. The change from a heavily structured architecture to a distributed one with novel uses of artificial intelligence, while not a silver bullet, and machine learning methods need to be further investigated. These include all layers from the PHY layer and above. There is another change that may need considerable discussion in regulatory bodies, which is the role of operators and spectrum allocation. Given that more services will be needed locally with low latency and high capacity and short coverage distances of mmWave systems, it is possible that it may be logical to have local operators covering certain premises rather than the macro level larger area. This talk will discuss these issues, current scenario in standardization and research leading into 6G.
